Operations will be able to be completed without significant disruption or issue based on current model runs and observations.

## Non-Technical Discussion
Based upon current model runs and observations, the probability for a high heat impact cannot be ruled out. Dewpoints may be reaching temperatures close to 80, and with temperatures falling close to and around the actual temperature, it is entirely possible the chance for fog exists, especially if any rain occurs during the afternoon, evening, or beginning night hours. With the development of fog, should the temperature drop below the dewpoint, condensation may pose a risk to radio and antenna equipment. However, due to the limited ability to accurately forecast this far in advance, a low risk for fog and condensation was omitted. 

Further, the possibility for high heat indexes is something which may be become a cause for concern closer to Field Day. Depending on how current trends continue, the introduction of a low operational hazard index may be necessary, with the adding of heat, fog, and/or condensation hazards becoming possible.

Current thinking is heat is the most likely threat among these. 

## Technical Discussion
The recent long-term GFS and ECWMF runs as of 00z June 8th indicate steadily increasing heat index values across the Florida peninsula, with heat index values breaching past 100, and into 105. This isn't necessarily guaranteed to happen, especially this far out, but the hazard is being considered and kept in mind as Field Day approaches. Consistently, the jet stream is anticipated to remain north of our location, and without the arrival of any temperature or heat relief, typically of that in the form of lows, it is possible that heat could compound. Any high pressures which remain over the state will be able to further elevate the heat indexes.

Dewpoints have been noted as reaching into the mid to even high 70s for some portions of the state, and if this scenario is to occur, actual temperatures will near the dewpoint, in some cases with only 2 to 3 degrees of separation, enough for the development of fog. This was noted as a progression over the course of several days, from approximately 230 to 360 hours in the forecast. 

Of course, remembering that such a situation is subject to the models being accurate this far out, the situation cannot yet be guaranteed to be a heat producer, and thus, the introduction of any hazard levels was withheld.
