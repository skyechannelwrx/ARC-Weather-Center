## Discussion
The severe weather risk currently remains low. This is likely the case based upon the high pressure ridging across the eastern United States which is forcing the jet stream north in a general blocking pattern, as has been the case since the last monthly outlook which was issued on May 31st. 

Although this high pressure is building and preventing any significant severe weather event, it is also contributing to an actively forming heat dome across the northeastern, eastern, and southeastern United States, which has then also contributed to elevated heat indexes, and seems likely to continue doing so to approximately July 8th, as was detailed in the post regarding poor weather conditions persisting into early and mid July. Although actual determination for this threat is subject to what conditions will be like for any particular event, it is safe to say that until at least July 8th, conditions will continue to be poor. 

The Climate Prediction Center outlines that, for both the monthly and seasonal outlooks for July and for Florida, we are sitting at an equal chance for both above or below normal precipitation for the month of July. Notably, this past June has been less active and less severe then last year, and thunderstorms are considerably less persistent than they were, and less often are storms going severe. This is to our favor, the general thunderstorm threat may be less than it was last year. With equal chances for above or below normal precipiation this month, we may or may not see significant impacts to outdoor operations. Even so, the models depict constant convection for the month of July based upon recent runs, and thus, this could become problematic to any outdoor operations if they occur during the afternoon to evening hours. 

We are also in a leaning above normal chance for above average temperatures, meaning there is a chance for temperatures to be hotter than normal. This should be kept in mind when planning for any POTAs or other outdoor events for this month. 

A Moderate El Nino is currently active, as the Nino3.4 index has reached 1.0+ degrees celsius. This is earlier than what was expected. The next milestone is a Strong El Nino, which requires a Nino3.4 index of 1.5+ degrees celsius.
