## Operational Summary
**...HIGH HEAT RISK...**

A high heat risk is  likely during Field Day. Those operating outdoors between the hours of 11am to 5pm, on the 26th, 27th, and 28th, should be aware of the potential for extreme heat. High heat index values of up to 102 are possible. Heat index values could go higher; the potential exists for heat index values up to 105, which would warrant the issuance of a Moderate risk.

Please refer to the Mitigation section for safety information.

## Mitigation
**...HIGH HEAT RISK PREPAREDNESS...**

High heat index values of up to 102, and possibly up to 105, are possible on the 26th, 27th, and 28th. These heat index values are hazardous, and could result in heat exshaustion or heat stroke if proper care is not exercised during the peak heating hours, which are between 11am to 5pm. 

Wear light, loose-fitting clothing. Drink plenty of water. Stay out of the sun when possible, and avoid strenuous activity through the peak heating hours of the day. Wear light colored clothing, dark colored clothing may absorb more heat from the sun. Return to an air conditioned room or vehicle if the heat becomes unbearable.

## Discussion
**...HIGH HEAT RISK...**

The high heat risk threat for Field Day has prompted the issuance of a Low risk. This low risk is for hazardous heat index values up to 102. However, it is possible heat index values could exceed this, and reach up to 105. If this is the case, a Moderate risk would be required for a Moderate hazard. Even if a Moderate risk is issued, however, mitigation would only be the necessary requirement as it relates to this high heat risk. 

**...FOG AND CONDENSATION...**

Using the National Digital Forecast Database, as well as the HRRR, NAM, RRFS, and NAM 3km, it's been determined conditions are unfavorable for the development of fog and condensation. The dewpoint is not expected to be within the acceptable range for fog to form. **There is a possibility for fog to still occur, but this probability appears low.**

**...THUNDERSTORMS AND RAIN...**

Variability in the existence of thunderstorms across the Florida peninsula over the 26th, 27th, and 28th is making it harder to determine if thunderstorms may pose a threat to outdoor operations. Even so, the biggest time period for thunderstorm organization appears to be on the 26th to the 27th, with the highest potential for thunderstorms existing on the 26th. The 27th and 26th have both been outlined under the National Digital Forecast Database as having a 30-40% probability of thunderstorms, but variability among the NAM 3km makes this iffy, but the other models appear to have sufficient convection which would warrant concern. An upgrade to a Moderate based on this variable alone may be necessary, but a decision on the upgrade will be withheld until tomorrow. 

**...WIND THREAT...**

It appears no sufficient wind threat exists to outdoor operations. However, with the existence of thunderstorms, any storms which become strong will be capable of producing locally gusty winds up to 55 MPH, and it is possible for a storm or two to become severe. Any severe storms will be capable of producing locally gusty winds up to 60 MPH.

**...AIR QUALITY...**

An air quality threat may exist. A large plume of saharran dust will make its way over the southern portions of the Florida peninsula. This dust may decrease the air quality of the local atmosphere. If this threat is to occur, those with sensitivity to allergens or poor air quality should be aware of the existence of degraded air.
