## Discussion 

The Climate Prediction Center has outlined that El Nino is likely to emerge soon, with an 86% chance likelihood through May-June-July, and anticipates this to remain, with a 96% chance, through to winter. Given this forecast, it is likely we may need to anticipate degraded conditions during Winter Field Day, such as potentially heavy rainfall, and in a worst case scenario, severe weather. 

Severe weather potential through to at least June 10th remains incredibly low, and not at all possible. An Omega Blocking pattern (that is, the jet stream is moving in the shape of the greek letter Omega) is routing most of the jet stream northward of the country into and along the US/Canadian border. This currently favors us, as we can at least be aware that the potential for organized severe weather is limited. However, due to the time of year we are in, the possibility for rainfall and pulse severe thunderstorms exists, especially as we progress into the summer months. June may become more potent for this reason.

Further, from now through to approximately June 8th, heavy precipitation is anticipated, and will become unfavorable for outdoor activities. 

Tomorrow begins hurricane season. We are currently monitoring a tropical wave located in the Caribbean, which is anticipated to travel northward and be guided into Florida by the jet stream. This portion of the jet stream is not anticipated to bring any severe weather threat. Or, if it does, a marginal threat. This system is likely the trigger for the moderate heavy rainfall chances across the peninsula. For those who are tabling, they should consider bringing an umbrella or rain coat during the dates through June 6th to June 8th while navigating outdoors.

This is not of great concern; but the potential for thunderstorms likely exists, as does rainfall, due to the nature of Florida's summer months and beginning rainy season. Mid-May typically marks the beginning of this rainy season, where it then typically peaks we head into July/August, and persists into September and October, before beginning to decline. However, if El Nino is favored, increased precipitation chances with developing mid-latitude cyclones that move through the Gulf and across to the Florida peninsula will become the greatest concern, as they may produce widespread high winds, severe weather, including the possibility for damaging tornadoes, large hail, and severe, damaging wind. However, it is too early to tell if this impact will occur, and likely relies on how intense this El Nino becomes.
