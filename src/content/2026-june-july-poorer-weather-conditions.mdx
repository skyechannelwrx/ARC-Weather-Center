Poor weather conditions are expected to persist into early and possibly mid July. These poor weather conditions include high heat and thunderstorms, with heat index values possibly up to 110, or passing it, which would require the issuance of a High risk. Further, thunderstorms will likely be persistent and present across most days during July, and conditions may become briefly worsened should the arrival of a tropical low or mid-latitude cyclone occur from the system currently located off the eastern United States.

## Discussion
As we progress into early and mid July, weather conditions are expected to remain poor. These poor weather conditions will likely frequently require Low to Moderate risks, though in some cases, the possibility for a High risk may be present either based on **high heat** or **thunderstorms.** Currently, the Climate Prediction Center has Florida outlined for the possibility of extreme heat out until July 8th.

Primarily, as we are full swing into the Florida summer, weather conditions will remain poor, and will consistently involve high heat risks with heat index values up to 110, or exceeding that, as well as isolated, to scattered, and occassionally widespread thunderstorms. Based upon recent trends, the potential for a few severe weather days, although the highest risk would is only expected to be Marginal, is also present with any organized severe weather events that could occur, primarily of which involves wind shear. 

The ECWMF and GFS both indicate continued convection approximately into the middle of this month. Based upon the progession of the tropical low currently located off the eastern United States coast, we could see even higher probabilities for rain and even the chance for a brief period of increased wind. Regardless of if the tropical low either evolves into a tropical system or becomes a mid-latitude cyclone (or a Noreaster), wind and rain would become a primary threat if the system meanders toward Florida, regardless of if it moves toward Northern or Southern Florida. These mid-latitude cyclones also could carry with them the possibility for severe weather, based on the general motion of the system. Steering winds would need to be present to guide the system into Florida, of which, based upon recent models, does not seem to be very present. 

The high pressure which is currently building in the eastern United States will remain and is not likely to weaken for some time. This high pressure will guide the jet stream into a blocking pattern which pushes the jet stream north, as has been the case for the past month or so. This is expected to continue. 

**Mitigation action** for this extreme heat and for thunderstorms has a few possibilities, of which are:

* Monitor the weather for thunderstorms, and wear light and loose fitting clothing, and stick to the shade where possible, as well as having water nearby and to remember to drink plenty of water
* Alternatively, POTAs or other operations could be conducted outside of peak thunderstorm and heat times, which would either be in the early morning, before 12pm, or later in the night, from approximately 6pm onward

Either scenario is up to individual preference, and both are good for mitigating both the extreme heat and thunderstorm risk.

Even so, there may be days, or otherwise gaps, in the extreme heat and thunderstorm likelihood. These days may be good days to consider outdoor operations, such as POTAs, and may be free from any hazard risk. But, this is subject to probability and predictability, and the chances for any particular day, especially during this time of year, to not have any hazard risk is considered to be low. 

Therefore, it may be best to hold off on outdoor activity until temperatures become cooler, or to prepare to mitigate any extreme heat or thunderstorm risk.
