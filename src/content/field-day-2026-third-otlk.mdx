## Operational Summary
Operations are still go with within comfortable parameters as of current, but operators should remain aware of the possibility for developing hazards as we get closer to Field Day.

## Mitigation
No mitigation action is needed at this time.

## Discussion
The GFS and ECWMF, as well as the GEFS and ENS, are both diverging in outputs from each other. The GEFS, primarily, supports the GFS, while the ENS supports the ECWMF. Based on the deterministic and ensemble outputs, either one favors outcomes which may impact Field Day operations. Notably, the GEFS and GFS do not support an outcome which necessitates the issuance of a low operational hazard risk. However, the ECWMF and ENS depict outcomes which may favor the need for a risk issuance based upon either condensation, fog, high heat index, rain, and even substantiates the possibility for a wind-driven threat. 

The GEFS supports wind gusts at approximately 10 to 13 mph, with the max wind gust from the ensemble members in the 20 mph range, which would require the issuance of a low risk. The GEFS also supports the condition for the issuance of a low risk based on precipitation, as it favors a higher chance of precipitation greater than 0.1 inches, but less than 1 inch.

The ENS supports wind gusts at the mean level up to 20 mph and, in some circumstances, exceeding that. Mean output also generated wind gusts up to 25 mph north of us. Max wind gust output from the ENS members was up to 25, and up to 38 north of us. Probability of this occurrence is low. However, based on both the wind outputs from the ENS and GEFS, including the max output from the GEFS, the probability of a low risk based on wind is supported, and these factors seem to warrant an increased likelihood of needing a low risk issuance. However, nothing appears to encroach on Moderate or High territory, and thus, the issuance of a Moderate or High risk is very unlikely. 

Monitoring of the SAL (Saharran Air Layer) is being conducted as it relates to air quality, for the purposes of ensuring suitable air quality conditions. The SAL is not currently forecasted to move inland to Florida, but should the SAL be pushed over the Florida peninsula, precipitation chances will diminish. 

Based upon this, no hazard can yet be ruled out. We are monitoring for the possibility of a heat risk, fog, condensation, precipitation, and potentially a low-end wind threat to equipment. 20 mph is the minimum wind speed required for a low risk wind threat, as it is enough to push over any antennas which are not properly fastened.

Because of the lack of agreement between the forecast models, no increase in confidence is being issued, as there is no telling for sure what hazards will be the most likely at the moment the day of Field Day. As June 26th, 27th, and 28th move out of the 300 hour range, confidence will increase. It is anticipated the highest confidence will be ascertained when the convective-allowing models are within range of the event.

The next forecast for Field Day 2026 will be issued June 18th, 2026, by 12pm EDT.
